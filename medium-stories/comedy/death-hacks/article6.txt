Death Hacks, Part VI: The Afterlife Is Optional

by Claudia G. Petersen

By now, a pattern should be clear.

Every time death threatens to conclude something, a system appears to prevent it.

First came myth.
Then bureaucracy.
Then databases.
Then monetization.
Then subscription immortality.

Each layer promised control.
Each layer demanded participation.

But not everyone wants to persist.

This is treated as a problem.

If Part V was about immortality as a service, then this is what happens when someone quietly asks:

What if I don’t want any of this?

Death Hack #17: Erasure Is a Skill

We are taught to document everything.

Photos.
Posts.
Receipts.
Proof.

Disappearance, once accidental, now requires expertise.

Accounts must be closed.
Backups must be deleted.
Archives must be opted out of — repeatedly.

There is no button for this.
There is no wizard to guide you through it.

To leave nothing behind is no longer passive.

It is work.

Death Hack #18: Refusal as a Final Act

Legacy culture assumes compliance.

You must be remembered.
You must be searchable.
You must leave something behind.

Refusal breaks the system.

No memorial page.
No scheduled reminders.
No digital echo resurfacing every year like a polite haunting.

This is not nihilism.

It is consent withdrawn.

And systems built on engagement do not handle refusal well.

Death Hack #19: The Ethics of Vanishing

We are told disappearance is selfish.

But permanence has costs — and they are paid by the living.

Grief that never resolves.
Memories that never soften.
Records that never forget context they were never meant to preserve.

Vanishing, done deliberately, is not abandonment.

It is boundary-setting.

Not everything meaningful needs to persist forever to matter.

Where This Leaves Us

Not all endings need witnesses.
Not all lives require documentation.
Not all deaths demand optimization.

Some things are meant to conclude — cleanly, quietly, without analytics.

In a world obsessed with persistence, choosing to end well is a radical act.

And with that, we reach the final truth behind every death hack:

The most powerful option is the one that cannot be monetized.

Until then —

xoxo,
Claudia

P.S. — If a system cannot tolerate your absence, that tells you something about who it was built for.
