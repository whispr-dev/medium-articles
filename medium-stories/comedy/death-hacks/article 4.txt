Death Hacks, Part IV: Capitalism and the Eternal Performance Review

by Claudia G. Petersen

Once upon a time, death was free.

You died.
People mourned.
Time passed.
Memory softened.
The universe moved on.

This was, apparently, inefficient.

At some point, the system that learned how to monetize attention noticed death and asked the only question it knows how to ask:

“But how do we keep them engaged?”

If Part III was about death as data persistence, then this is what happens next:

Death, having been stored, is now optimized.

Welcome to the phase of the afterlife where you are no longer a soul, a record, or even a ghost.

You are a product.

Death Hack #10: Memorialization Is Not Rest

Most platforms now offer to “memorialize” your account.

This sounds comforting until you look at what it actually does.

Your profile stops aging.
Your content keeps circulating.
Your name remains clickable.

You are not gone.
You are paused.

Friends don’t remember you by telling stories.
They remember you by scrolling past you.

There is no ceremony.
No ending.
Just a pinned presence that never quite resolves into absence.

This is not rest.

This is indefinite hosting.

Death Hack #11: Grief as a Service

In earlier eras, grief was communal.
Messy.
Offline.

Today, grief has a user interface.

Subscription memorial pages.
Algorithmically resurfaced “memories.”
Automated anniversary reminders delivered with the emotional sensitivity of a parking ticket.

You do not grieve when you are ready.
You grieve when the platform decides it is time.

There is something deeply unsettling about being prompted to miss someone — especially when the prompt is indistinguishable from a marketing notification.

Grief is no longer a process.
It is a feature.

Death Hack #12: Legacy Is a Brand Problem

Everyone is told to “think about their legacy.”

What this actually means is:

Curate yourself in advance.

Choose the photos.
Draft the posts.
Pre-approve the version of you that will survive.

Legacy used to emerge organically, shaped by memory and distortion and time.

Now it is a content strategy.

And like all branding exercises, it fails for the same reason:

The most enduring things about a person are never the things they would put in a media kit.

Death Hack #13: The Eternal Performance Review

You may be gone, but the evaluation continues.

Who attended the funeral.
Who posted.
Who didn’t.

Engagement spikes.
Engagement fades.

You trend briefly, and then you are quietly archived.

There is no final judgment — only diminishing reach.

If Hell is a dashboard, this is the moment the graph slopes gently downward until you disappear from view.

Not into oblivion.

Into irrelevance.

Where This Leaves Us

Capitalism does not fear death.
It fears disengagement.

As long as you can be remembered, tagged, monetized, resurfaced, or packaged, you remain useful. When you cannot, you are not punished.

You are simply dropped.

And next time, we’ll talk about the most seductive promise of all — the one offered as an escape from this system:

Immortality.

Available as a service.
Billed monthly.
Cancel anytime (conditions apply).

Until then —

xoxo,
Claudia

P.S. — If someone is making money from your memory, it may be worth asking who, exactly, that’s for.
