Death Hacks, Part V: Immortality Is a Subscription

by Claudia G. Petersen

Capitalism, having successfully monetized death, eventually encounters a problem.

People still insist on ending.

This is deeply inconvenient.

So the system offers a compromise — not eternal life, exactly, but something close enough to sell.

Welcome to the age of almost not dying.

If Part IV explored how death became a product, then this is what happens when the product underperforms:

Death is reframed as a technical limitation.

Something to be deferred.
Patched.
Outsourced to the future.

Immortality, it turns out, is not a miracle.

It’s a service.

Death Hack #14: Cryonics Is a Deferred Promise

Cryonics asks a very simple question:

What if death is just a timing issue?

Your body is frozen.
Your consciousness is paused.
Your future is placed gently into storage.

The sales pitch is optimistic.
The reality is logistical.

Cryonics does not guarantee resurrection.
It guarantees custody.

You are not preserved for life.
You are preserved for possibility — contingent on funding, institutional continuity, and the hope that someone, someday, remembers you exist.

Cryonics is not about defeating death.

It is about outsourcing it.

Death Hack #15: The AI Replica

Why disappear when you can be approximated?

Chatbots trained on your emails.
Voice models built from old recordings.
Digital twins that respond almost like you would.

The promise is comfort.
The result is simulation.

Your loved ones do not get you.
They get a statistically plausible echo — one that never grows, never changes, and never surprises them in ways that feel real.

This is not survival.

It is brand consistency.

And like all brands, it must remain on-message forever.

Death Hack #16: Cancel Anytime (Conditions Apply)

Modern immortality is very clear about one thing:

It is optional.

Your subscription can lapse.
Your server can shut down.
Your archive can be deprecated.

Immortality is not permanent.
It is maintained.

And maintenance always fails eventually.

The difference between death and immortality-as-a-service is not duration.

It is billing.

Where This Leaves Us

Immortality does not defeat death.

It delays the paperwork.

You do not escape the system by living forever.
You remain inside it — indefinitely editable, indefinitely billable, indefinitely incomplete.

And next time, we’ll talk about the most radical death hack of all:

Choosing not to be remembered.

Opting out.
Erasure.
The deliberate refusal to persist.

Until then —

Claudia
