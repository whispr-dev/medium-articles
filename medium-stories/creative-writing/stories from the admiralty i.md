**EXCLUSIVE: INSIDE THE NEW ADMIRALTY — A PALACE OF IRON AND PURPOSE!!!**
Your correspondent was granted extraordinary access to the newly restored Admiralty building, where work proceeds day and night under the tireless direction of Admiral Metrot himself. The great hall, once fallen to dust and bureaucratic neglect, now rings with the sound of industry. Charts cover every surface. Young officers dash to and fro with the urgent purpose of men who know they are building something that will outlast them. "We do not rebuild for ourselves," Admiral Metrot told this reporter, his famous Malamute Kodiak seated at his feet. "We rebuild for those who will come after. For the child not yet born who will one day stand upon the deck of a ship bearing the White Ensign, and know that he is part of something eternal."
- c.1924 Feb 28; The Illustrated London News, special supplement: "The Restoration of British Sea Power."

_I must tell you of the First Expedition, for I was there—a junior lieutenant, barely twenty-two, utterly terrified and utterly alive. We sailed for the Antarctic in the spring of 1924, a fleet of seven vessels under Admiral Metrot's personal command. The ostensible purpose was scientific: to chart the coastline, to collect specimens, to plant the flag. But we all knew the true purpose. We were there to prove that the Admiralty lived again. We were there to show the world that Britain still commanded the seas. The Malamutes came with us—all seventeen of them, and old Kodiak besides, though he was grey about the muzzle and slow in his movements. The Admiral would not sail without them. "They found me in the ice," he said, "and I shall not abandon them to the warmth of England."_
- c.1967; recorded oral testimony of Vice Admiral Sir Geoffrey Craine, interviewed for the Imperial War Museum archives, tape 7 of 12.

_I do not speak often of what happened on the ice shelf. The official reports describe it as a "minor incident." Those of us who were there know differently. The Admiral was sixty-three years old. The water was thirty-one degrees below freezing. Kodiak weighed one hundred and forty pounds and was thrashing in blind panic. Any sensible man would have let the dog go. Troy Metrot went into that water without hesitation. Without thought. He went in, and he brought that dog out, and when we hauled them both onto the ice, the Admiral was blue-lipped and barely conscious, but his arms were locked around Kodiak's chest and nothing on God's earth could have made him let go. "He saved me once," the Admiral said later, from his cot in the sick bay, the dog pressed against his side for warmth. "One does not forget such things."_
- c.1924 Jun 18; from the private diary of Surgeon-Lieutenant Marcus Thorne, HMS Indomitable, Antarctic Expedition.

_And so I come to the end of my tale, though the tale itself has no ending—for the Admiralty endures, as it was always meant to endure, and the ships still sail under the White Ensign, and somewhere, in every generation, there is a young officer who looks out at the grey Atlantic and feels the stirring of something ancient and unnameable in his breast. Admiral Metrot died in 1931, at the age of seventy. Kodiak had preceded him by three years, and the Admiral was never quite the same afterward. He was buried at sea, as he had requested, off the coast of Victoria Land—the territory he had claimed for the Crown, in the place where he had nearly died to save his friend. The seventeen Malamutes, and their descendants, remain to this day at the Admiralty, where they are cared for by a dedicated staff and treated with the reverence due to national treasures. They still howl, sometimes, on winter nights. The young officers say they are calling to the Admiral, across the ice, across the years, across the veil of death itself. I do not know if this is true. But I like to believe it._
- c.1952; from "The Frozen Admiral: A Life of Troy Metrot" by Harriet Pembury-Shaw, Faber & Faber, Epilogue: "The Long Voyage Home."
