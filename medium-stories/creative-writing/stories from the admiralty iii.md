STORIES FROM THE ADMIRALTY III: THE UNMARKED SHIP

\_Being a collected record of Commander (later Vice Admiral Sir) Francis Bellamy-Court DSO DSC RN, HMS Dauntless, and the North Atlantic, 1939–1949: in documents, dispatches, and remembrances\_



\_Three years a peacetime navy, and now this. We were told at 1100 hours. I stood the crew on deck and read them the declaration, and there was a moment — perhaps five seconds — of complete silence aboard HMS Dauntless, as if the ship herself were considering it. Then Leading Seaman Tufnell said, "Right then, sir," and men began to move, and that was the end of peace.\_



\_Oslo came up from below at some point during this and sat on the capstan and watched with great attention, as though she understood the gravity of the occasion. She is a Norwegian Forest Cat, grey and enormous, acquired in Bergen in '37 when she was a kitten and a midshipman I shall not name smuggled her aboard in his cap. She has been the unofficial senior rating of this vessel for two years. I have never in my life been as certain of anything as Oslo is certain of everything.\_



* c.1939 Sept 3; private journal of Commander Francis Bellamy-Court RN, HMS Dauntless, berthed Portsmouth.

\_The water temperature was thirty-one degrees Fahrenheit. A man in that water has perhaps fifteen minutes of useful consciousness. We had eleven minutes from the sinking to my order to break formation. I calculated that we had, at the outside, four minutes to reach survivors who were still alive and capable of surviving rescue.\_



\_I did not, at that moment, consult the Admiralty regulations regarding convoy escort procedure. I consulted the men in the water.\_



\_There were thirty-one of them. We recovered twenty-nine. The other two we could not reach in time, and I will carry that for the rest of my life, as I suppose I must. But I will not carry twenty-nine.\_



* c.1955; from "My Ship, My Command: A Naval Memoir" by Vice Admiral Sir Francis Bellamy-Court DSO DSC, Heinemann, Chapter IX: "The Haraldsen."



\*\*DISPATCH — MOST SECRET — FOR ADMIRALTY EYES ONLY\*\*

\*\*FROM:\*\* NAVAL INTELLIGENCE DIVISION

\*\*TO:\*\* FIRST SEA LORD

\*\*DATE:\*\* 22 NOVEMBER 1941



Among the survivors recovered from SS Haraldsen by HMS Dauntless on the night of 17/18 November is one Major Eirik Hagen (Norwegian Army, attached SIS). Major Hagen was travelling under civilian cover with documents of the utmost intelligence value. These documents, which survived immersion in waterproof packaging, are now in the possession of NID and are currently under analysis. Preliminary assessment suggests significant operational relevance to current cryptographic priorities. Full assessment to follow.



Commander Bellamy-Court is unaware of the above. Classification: MOST SECRET.



I did not know about the intelligence officer. I want to be absolutely clear about that. If I had known, it would have made no difference to my decision — I trust I would have acted the same regardless — but I would have preferred not to know even in retrospect, because it makes the thing tidier than it was, and the thing was not tidy. It was thirty-one people in thirty-one-degree water in the North Atlantic at night, and I went in to get them. That's the whole of it.



* c.1968; recorded oral testimony of Vice Admiral Sir Francis Bellamy-Court, interviewed for the Imperial War Museum archives, reel 2 of 6.



\*\*ADMIRALTY NOTICE — FOR IMMEDIATE DISTRIBUTION TO ALL ESCORT COMMANDERS\*\*



It has been brought to the attention of the Admiralty that on the night of 17/18 November, Commander F. Bellamy-Court RN, in command of HMS Dauntless, wilfully and without authorisation broke formation during active anti-submarine operations in contravention of Standing Convoy Escort Order 14(b). Commander Bellamy-Court is hereby relieved of his command pending a formal Court of Inquiry. HMS Dauntless to be temporarily assigned to Commander R.P. Henshaw RN.



* c.1941 Dec 2; Admiralty Notice B/177/41.



\*\*DESTROYER COMMANDER RELIEVED OF COMMAND AFTER DEFYING ORDERS IN NORTH ATLANTIC!!!\*\*



Commander Francis Bellamy-Court RN has been relieved of his command and faces a formal Court of Inquiry following his unauthorised decision to break convoy escort formation on the night of November 17th in order to rescue survivors from the torpedoed Norwegian vessel SS Haraldsen. The Commodore commanding Escort Group B is understood to have twice ordered Commander Bellamy-Court to return to screen. Commander Bellamy-Court disobeyed both orders. Twenty-nine survivors were recovered.



* c.1941 Dec 5; The Times of London.



\_She does not understand why she is no longer on the ship. Oslo, I mean. When they came for me — two perfectly polite sub-lieutenants with an equally polite letter — she was on the bridge, as she usually is at that hour, watching the water. She watched us go with an expression I can only describe as judicial. I had to carry her down the gangplank myself.\_



\_She is sitting on the desk now, between me and this journal, staring at me in the way that she does when she finds my behaviour inadequate. She is, I think, the most honest critic I possess.\_



* c.1941 Dec 3; private journal of Commander Francis Bellamy-Court, naval accommodation, Portsmouth.



Court of Inquiry

COURT OF INQUIRY INTO THE CONDUCT OF COMMANDER F. BELLAMY-COURT RN

HMS EXCELLENT, PORTSMOUTH — 14 JANUARY 1942

TRANSCRIPT — PARTIAL — CLASSIFICATION: RESTRICTED



PRESIDENT OF THE COURT: Admiral Sir George Pembury-Wace KCB

MEMBERS: Rear Admiral Stanton-Loach CB; Captain J.H. Carrow DSO RN



\_ADMIRAL PEMBURY-WACE: Commander Bellamy-Court, you have heard the charges as read. How do you answer them?\_



\_BELLAMY-COURT: I answer them honestly, sir. I broke formation. I disobeyed a direct order from the Escort Commander. I did so in full knowledge of what I was doing and in full acceptance of the consequences.\_



\_PEMBURY-WACE: You do not deny the charges.\_



\_BELLAMY-COURT: I deny nothing. I explain everything.\_



\_PEMBURY-WACE: Then explain.\_



\_BELLAMY-COURT: There were thirty-one men and women in the water, sir. The water temperature was thirty-one degrees Fahrenheit. They had, at that moment, perhaps four minutes. I was the nearest vessel. I had the means to save them. I saved twenty-nine of them. I am prepared to discuss my methods at whatever length the court requires. I am not prepared to pretend that I would decide differently.\_



\_REAR ADMIRAL STANTON-LOACH: Commander, you are aware that your action allowed a U-boat to escape.\_



\_BELLAMY-COURT: I am aware that the U-boat was not caught, sir. Whether my presence in the convoy screen would have changed that outcome I cannot say with any certainty.\_



\_STANTON-LOACH: You are not here to adjudicate on the probabilities, Commander.\_



\_BELLAMY-COURT: With respect, sir, in the North Atlantic in November, one is always adjudicating on probabilities.\_



\_CAPTAIN CARROW: Commander. Did you at any point consider that your action might endanger the remaining vessels in the convoy?\_



\_BELLAMY-COURT: I considered it, Captain. I concluded that the screen had six remaining escorts and that my temporary absence, for the time required to effect rescue, did not materially increase the risk to the convoy. I may have been wrong. I judged that I was not.\_



\_PEMBURY-WACE: You judged. Against the explicit order of your superior officer.\_



\_BELLAMY-COURT: Yes, sir.\_



\_PEMBURY-WACE: On what authority?\_



\_BELLAMY-COURT:\_ \[pause] \_On the authority of the thirty-one people in the water, sir. I'm afraid I don't have a better answer than that.\_



\[PAUSE — NOTED IN TRANSCRIPT]



\_PEMBURY-WACE: The Court will adjourn.\_



\_My dear Frank,\_



\_Mama and I heard it on the wireless. The boys don't know yet — I've told them that Daddy's ship is in for repairs and that he is taking a holiday, which Thomas finds suspicious and William accepts entirely because he is four and has no reason to disbelieve it.\_



\_I want you to know that I am not afraid, and that I am not ashamed, and that whatever those men in that room decide about the regulations, I know what you are. Come home to us soon.\_



\_All my love, always,\_

\_Margaret\_



* c.1942 Jan 16; personal correspondence, Bellamy-Court family archive.



COURT OF INQUIRY — FINDING — 22 JANUARY 1942 — RESTRICTED



The Court finds that Commander F. Bellamy-Court RN did, on the night of 17/18 November 1941, act in contravention of Standing Convoy Escort Order 14(b) by breaking formation without authorisation and disregarding two direct orders from his superior officer. These are serious matters which this Court does not minimise.



The Court further finds that Commander Bellamy-Court's action resulted in the recovery of twenty-nine survivors from SS Haraldsen who would, in all reasonable probability, have perished.



The Court further notes, in terms that cannot at present be made public, that information has been placed before it — by direction of the Naval Intelligence Division, with the authority of the First Sea Lord — which materially affects the assessment of this incident. The Court is not at liberty to specify the nature of this information, except to record that it is of the highest significance to current operations.



The Court's finding is as follows: Commander Bellamy-Court acted in breach of orders. Commander Bellamy-Court is, nonetheless, to be restored to command without censure. The Court notes, for the record and for the instruction of future officers, that the navigation of difficult moral waters requires a compass that regulations alone cannot always provide. Commander Bellamy-Court's compass, on the night in question, did not fail him.



Signed: \_Admiral Sir George Pembury-Wace KCB, President.\_

\_Oslo died in the spring of 1949, in the garden of our house in Lyme Regis, which is where she had decided to retire, and which she patrolled with the same comprehensive authority she had once applied to the North Atlantic. She was approximately fourteen years old. We buried her under the apple tree.\_



\_I find I cannot write more than that. Some losses reduce one to simplicity.\_



* c.1955; from "My Ship, My Command," Author's Note.



\_He was the most complete naval officer I ever served under, and I served under seven. What made him different wasn't the heroism — the North Atlantic, Normandy, all of it — it was the quality of his attention. He paid attention to his men in the way that a great doctor pays attention to a patient: not just to the symptom but to the whole person. He remembered birthdays. He remembered the names of wives and children. He remembered that Tufnell's boy had been ill and asked after him three weeks later. Small things. But a man who is paid that quality of attention will follow you anywhere, and Dauntless' men would have followed Bellamy-Court to the floor of the ocean and back up again.\_



\_The other thing I'll say is this: Oslo would sleep on any man aboard when she was in the mood for it. But she only ever slept on his lap. Cats, in my experience, know.\_



* c.1971; recorded oral testimony of Chief Petty Officer Arthur Maginty (retd.), interviewed for the Imperial War Museum archives, reel 1 of 2.



\_Vice Admiral Sir Francis Bellamy-Court DSO DSC died in 1974, at the age of sixty-nine, of a heart that his doctors declared to be in remarkable condition for a man who had asked so much of it. He had served forty-one years in the Royal Navy, commanded five vessels, and trained a generation of officers who, to a man, remembered two things about him: that he would always make the right call in the worst moment, and that he would always, without fail, know your name.\_



\_The 1942 Court of Inquiry finding — the full, unredacted version — was declassified in 1973, one year before his death, when the intelligence operations surrounding the Haraldsen survivors could finally be discussed openly. Bellamy-Court was asked by a journalist that year whether the revelation had changed anything for him. He considered this for a long moment.\_



\_"It changes the story," he said. "It doesn't change what happened on the water."\_



\_He was buried with full naval honours at Portsmouth, where HMS Dauntless — long since decommissioned, her name now carried by a Type 45 destroyer — sent a guard of honour. A Norwegian naval attaché attended uninvited and unexpected, and placed flowers at the graveside on behalf of a government that had not forgotten. Nobody had asked him to come. He had come anyway.\_



\_Oslo, of course, was already there. The apple tree was in bloom.\_



* c.1976; from "The Unmarked Ship: A Life of Francis Bellamy-Court" by R.J. Alderton, John Murray, Epilogue.



And so the sea keeps its ledger in silence, and the inquiry transcripts yellow in the archive, and somewhere in the long institutional memory of the Admiralty — in a corridor where the portraits of the dead watch the living pass — there is a painting of a grey Norwegian Forest Cat on a wartime gangplank, waiting with absolute certainty for her man to come back.



She always knew he would.
