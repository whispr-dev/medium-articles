\_I will not say that I am not afraid. That would be a lie, and I have found that lies told to oneself are the most corrosive of all. I am afraid. The Pacific is the largest thing in the world and I am a very small aeroplane. But I have always found that fear, properly understood, is merely excitement that has forgotten its manners. My father told the Times that he wished I wouldn't. I told the Times that I wished he'd come and watch. He'll be at Croydon at dawn. He always is.\_



* c.1928 Oct 3; from the private journal of Captain Vivienne Ashmore-Bright, the night before her departure, Croydon Aerodrome.

Captain Vivienne Ashmore-Bright, 31, who departed Honolulu aerodrome at 0420 hours local time on Wednesday last, has failed to arrive at her scheduled waypoint of Pago Pago, nor has any wireless signal been received from her aircraft, the Caledonia III, since 0817 hours Wednesday morning. \_My Lord,\_



\_I write to you not as the father of a missing aviator but as a man I have known these thirty years, and I write plainly: you must go home. You must sleep. The searchers will not find her any sooner for your presence in this building. I know what you are feeling, for I have felt it myself — the particular terror of watching someone you love walk deliberately toward the horizon. But Vivienne knew the horizon. She chose it. She would not, I think, wish you to pace these corridors into ruin.\_



\_Come to dinner Thursday. Bring the dog.\_



\_Your friend always,

Aubrey Fentham-Cross,

Permanent Under-Secretary, Air Ministry\_



* c.1928 Oct 22; private correspondence, Ashmore-Bright family archive.



\_AIR MINISTRY OFFICIAL STATEMENT — Issued 14th November, 1928\_



\_After four weeks of extensive search operations conducted in partnership with the United States Navy and the Royal Australian Air Force, the Air Ministry has concluded that further active search for Captain Vivienne Ashmore-Bright and her aircraft Caledonia III is no longer operationally justifiable. Captain Ashmore-Bright is to be listed as missing, presumed lost, over the Central Pacific Ocean. She was, in every respect, an officer of the first order.\_

\_The aeroplane came down at night, which was the worst of it. I had the instruments and I had my training and I had the roaring certainty that nothing was where it should be, and then there was a great shock of white water and then nothing for a time. I came to on a beach. The Caledonia III was gone entirely. I had a knife, a compass, my flying jacket, and the contents of my breast pocket: a photograph of my father, a letter I had not yet posted, and a boiled sweet from the tin the ground crew at Honolulu had pressed upon me at departure. I ate the sweet immediately, which I have since decided was the correct decision.\_



\_The atoll was perhaps two miles at its longest dimension. There was fresh water — a miracle — and coconuts, and fish if I could catch them, and I could. I made a shelter. I kept a calendar on a palm trunk with my knife. I talked aloud to myself, which I am told is either a sign of sanity or its opposite, and I have never been entirely sure which.\_



\_The cat arrived in late November, clinging to a section of timber the sea delivered to my beach one morning with the casual generosity that the Pacific sometimes exhibited toward me. She was ginger, and thin, and absolutely furious about the whole situation. The timber bore a name in fragments of weathered paint that I eventually deciphered as MERIDIAN — a copra freighter lost in a squall six weeks prior. I called the cat Meridian. She was not grateful for the rescue, or for the name, but she stayed. She always stayed.\_



\_I will not say that the cat saved my life. That is the kind of thing one says. But I will say that there were mornings — many mornings — when the only reason I rose from my shelter and set about the serious business of surviving was that Meridian expected to be fed, and her expectations brooked no argument. One cannot, I found, despair effectively in the presence of a hungry cat.\_



* c.1931; from "The Long Sky: My Life in Aviation" by Captain Vivienne Ashmore-Bright, Victor Gollancz Ltd., Chapter XII: "The Atoll."



\_My dear Father,\_



\_I write this knowing you may never read it, and that is perhaps why I find I can write it honestly. I am alive. I intend to remain alive. This is a decision I renew each morning, and I find that the renewing of it keeps it fresh.\_



\_I think often of Croydon — of the way you stood at the edge of the aerodrome in the grey October light and did not cry, though I know it cost you. I think about what I said to you before I climbed in, which was something flippant and insufficient, as my things to you always are. What I meant was: I love you, and I am not afraid, and you were the reason I ever looked up at the sky in the first place.\_



\_There is a cat here. She is dreadful company and I adore her.\_



\_Your daughter always,

Vivienne\_



* c.1929 Jan \[undated]; letter found among Captain Ashmore-Bright's effects on the atoll, never sent; preserved in the Ashmore-Bright Archive, RAF Museum Hendon.



\_The crossing from Auckland was, they tell me, a matter of some public excitement. I was not aware of this, as I spent a considerable portion of it asleep. Meridian, for her part, took to the ship with equanimity and was discovered on the third day occupying the First Officer's chair on the bridge, which she apparently considered her due. The Navy, to their considerable credit, did not dispute this.\_



\_My father was at Southampton. He looked older, and I told him so, and he said that I looked like something the sea had thrown back, and we stood on the dock and laughed until we wept and wept until we laughed, and Meridian sat on my kit-bag and watched us with the expression of one who has seen considerably worse.\_



* c.1931; from "The Long Sky," Chapter XIII: "Southampton."

She did not rest. That was the thing about Vivienne — she was constitutionally incapable of it. Within six months of her return she was before the Air Ministry with a proposal: a permanent survey of Pacific air routes, to be undertaken by a dedicated service under civilian charter with military support. "The Pacific tried to kill me," she said at the committee. "The least I can do is map it properly so that the next person has a fighting chance."



The Pacific Air Survey was formally established in 1932. She ran it for seventeen years.

Vivienne Ashmore-Bright died in 1949, at the age of fifty-two, of a heart that had, her doctors said, simply worked too hard for too long. She had trained forty-seven pilots, charted eleven thousand miles of ocean, and in 1944, at the age of forty-seven, flown a supply run to a Pacific garrison under conditions that caused the Air Ministry to award her the Distinguished Flying Cross and then, in the most respectful terms possible, ask her never to do anything like that again.



Meridian died in 1938, in the warmth of the Survey's operations room at RAF Lyme, attended by three pilots and a Wing Commander who all maintained afterward, with great dignity, that they had not been crying. Every Survey cat that came after was named Meridian, and this tradition continues to the present day. There is always a Meridian at Pacific Air Survey headquarters.



Ashmore-Bright was buried at sea, as she had requested — south of the atoll, in the waters where the Caledonia III went down, twenty-one years before. The Navy gave her full honours. The current Meridian, a large and self-possessed tabby, sat on the deck throughout and watched the proceedings with the air of one who understood perfectly what was happening and had decided, on balance, that it was acceptable.



The survey flights still go out. The Pacific is still the largest thing in the world. But it is a little better known now, and a little less terrible, and somewhere in the long blue distances between the islands, the white line of a contrail catches the morning light and is gone.



* c.1952; from "The Long Sky: A Life of Vivienne Ashmore-Bright" by Clara Holt, Faber \& Faber, Epilogue: "The Wide Water."



\_And so the sky keeps its records in contrails and in memory, and the ocean keeps its secrets below, and between the two of them, now and always, there is a small bright point of light moving westward, westward, westward, into the sun.\_
