Better Living Through Undeserved Reputation Chemistry

by Claudia G. Petersen

There is a special circle of social hell reserved for chemicals with bad vibes.

They are not poisonous in the usual sense.
They are not especially dangerous.
They are, however, deeply untrendy.

These are the molecules that have been tried, convicted, and sentenced by public opinion — not because of what they do, but because of how they sound.

In this series, we’re going to talk about chemicals with undeserved reputations. Compounds that trigger immediate reactions — fear, disgust, certainty — despite being chemically mundane, biologically familiar, or actively beneficial.

This is not a defense of industry.
This is not a takedown of “natural living.”
This is not an argument for blind trust.

It’s something far more irritating:

It’s chemistry.

Chemistry doesn’t care about your aesthetic preferences. It doesn’t care whether something feels artificial, clean, traditional, modern, Western, Eastern, corporate, or wholesome. Molecules don’t know which marketing department introduced them to you.

They only know how to interact.

And again and again, we find the same pattern: the substances people fear most are not the ones doing the harm — they’re just the ones we’ve told the most compelling stories about.

So in the coming pieces, we’ll calmly unpack why certain chemicals became villains, what they actually do, and why our certainty about them often says more about us than about the science.

We’ll start with the undisputed champion of chemical misinformation.

Monosodium glutamate.
