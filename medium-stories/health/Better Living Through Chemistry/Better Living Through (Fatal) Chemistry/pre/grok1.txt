1. The Classics: WWI Choking & Blister Agents
The OG "Beta Test" of Chemical Warfare

World War I was effectively a massive, unethical open-beta for chemical weapons. These agents were crude, heavy, and relied on the wind (which famously backfired on the British at Loos, blowing the gas right back into their own trenches).
​

Chlorine (Cl₂)
The Smell: Pineapple and Pepper (weirdly specific, right?).
​

The Chemistry: A diatomic gas, green and heavy. It reacts with water in your lungs to form hydrochloric acid. You essentially drown in your own dissolved lung tissue.

The Hack: It’s water-soluble. Soldiers quickly figured out that breathing through a rag soaked in water (or, in desperate times, urine) would neutralize the chlorine before it hit the lungs. Ammonia in urine reacts with chlorine to form chloramines, which are less deadly.

Phosgene (CG)
The Smell: Musty Hay.

The Chemistry: Carbonyl dichloride. Much sneakier than chlorine. It doesn’t cause immediate coughing fits at low doses, so soldiers would keep breathing it in. The lethal effects (fluid filling the lungs) could take up to 48 hours to execute.
​

Factoid: It killed way more people than Chlorine (85% of chemical fatalities in WWI), but Chlorine gets all the press because it was first.
​

Mustard Gas (H)
The Smell: Garlic or Horseradish.

The Chemistry: A "vesicant" (blister agent). It’s not actually a gas, but a fine mist of oily liquid. It attacks DNA, causing massive blisters on skin and lungs.

The "Strange Surprise": The only silver lining in this cloud of misery? In 1943, a US ship carrying mustard gas bombs was destroyed in Bari, Italy. Doctors noticed the survivors had depleted white blood cells. This observation led directly to the invention of chemotherapy (mustine) to treat leukemia. We found a cure for cancer inside a weapon of mass destruction.
​

2. The G-Series: German Nerve Agents
"Oops, I Just Invented a WMD"

These were all discovered by accident in the 1930s/40s by German scientists looking for better pesticides. The "G" stands for German.
​

Tabun (GA)
The Smell: Faintly fruity (like bitter almonds).

The Origin: Discovered by Gerhard Schrader in 1936. He was trying to kill weevils, not people. A tiny drop fell on a lab bench, and he and his assistant spent weeks in the hospital struggling to see and breathe.
​

The Mechanism: It acts like a DDoS attack on your nervous system. It inhibits acetylcholinesterase, the "cleanup crew" enzyme. Without it, your nerves keep firing "CONTRACT!" signals to your muscles until they seize up and you stop breathing.

Sarin (GB)
The Smell: Odorless (making it the perfect assassin).

The Delivery: It's incredibly volatile (evaporates fast).

The Weird Delivery Hack: In the 1995 Tokyo Subway attack, the cult Aum Shinrikyo didn't use fancy missiles. They put liquid Sarin in plastic lunch bags, dropped them on the train floor, and poked them with sharpened umbrella tips before running away. Low-tech delivery for a high-tech poison.
​

Soman (GD) & Cyclosarin (GF)
The Smell: Soman smells like camphor (Vicks VapoRub) or rotting fruit; Cyclosarin smells like peaches.

The Threat: Soman "ages" the bond with your enzymes within minutes, meaning if you don't get the antidote essentially immediately, it becomes permanent.
​

3. The V-Series: Venomous Agents
The British "Upgrade"

If the G-series was v1.0, the V-series (Venomous) was the v2.0 patch developed by the British in the 1950s (specifically at Porton Down). They traded the tech to the USA in exchange for nuclear weapon secrets.
​

VX
The Structure: An amber, oily liquid. It is not volatile. It sticks to everything like an invisible, deadly motor oil.

Lethality: 10 milligrams on your skin is Game Over. It is the most toxic nerve agent ever synthesized.
​

The "Skull Valley" Incident: In 1968, a US Army jet tested a spray tank of VX in Utah. The valve got stuck open. The mist drifted downwind into a place called "Skull Valley" (you can't make this up) and killed 6,000 sheep. The army initially denied it until the dead sheep piled up.
​

Amiton (VG)
The Marketing Fail: This agent was actually brought to market as an insecticide in 1954. It was sold under the trade name "Tetram." They pulled it quickly when they realized it was just... too good at killing the farmers, not just the bugs. It was later reclassified as a nerve agent.
​

4. The Novichok Agents: The "Newcomers"
The Soviet Binary Code

Developed by the USSR/Russia from the 1970s-90s to bypass NATO detectors. "Novichok" means "Newcomer".
​

The Chemistry Hack
Binary Weapons: These agents were designed to be stored as two separate, less toxic powders or liquids. They only become deadly when mixed, usually right inside the weapon shell or sprayer. This made them safer to transport and harder for inspectors to find, as the ingredients themselves weren't necessarily banned.

The Form: Unlike the liquid/gas G and V agents, some Novichoks are solid ultra-fine powders. This changes the game for decontamination—you can't just wipe it away; the dust lingers everywhere.
​

Lethality: Reportedly 5-8 times more deadly than VX.

5. Riot Control: "Non-Lethal" Pain
The Spicy Stuff

These are designed to incapacitate, not terminate, but they are still chemically fascinating.
​

CS Gas (Tear Gas)
The Truth: It’s not a gas! It’s a white solid powder (2-chlorobenzalmalononitrile) that is burned to create a smoke, or dissolved in solvent to make a spray.

The Receptor Hack: It hijacks the TRPA1 receptor in your body—the same one that detects Wasabi and Horseradish. It tricks your body into thinking it’s essentially on fire.
​

The Milk Myth: People pour milk on their eyes to stop the burning. The files say this is mostly placebo; water or saline is just as effective (and less sticky). The fat in milk might help dissolve the crystals slightly better than water, but it's not a magic antidote.
​

Pepper Spray (OC)
The Molecule: Capsaicin. The exact same oil found in chili peppers, just concentrated to an insane degree. It’s "all-natural" suffering.
